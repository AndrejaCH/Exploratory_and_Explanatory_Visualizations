PISA 2012 - Effects of Parents Backgrounds on Students' Skills and Knowledge¶

by Andreja Ho¶

Investigation Overview¶

The purpose of this analysis is to investigate if parents backgrounds and whether students coming from countries that are part of OECD have an effect on students scores. The main focus is on the parent's highest education, wealth, cultural and home possessions, educational resources at home, single parenting , OECD country* and their effect on students' scores in mathematics, reading and science.

*OECD = Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development

Dataset Overview¶

PISA 2012 is a survey of students' skills and knowledge as they approach the end of compulsory education. Original PISA 2012 dataset is very complex and contains 485490 rows and 636 columns. For the purpose of this analysis I focused on only a few features, mentioned above. After cleaning the dataset contains 325497 rows and 19 columns.

Distribution of values for OECD Country, Gender and Single-Parents¶

Distribution of values for OECD countries and gender is pretty uniform. There are slightly more countries that joined OECD, that is 59.6% in comparison to countries that have not joined OECD 40.4%. There is an equal distribution for both genders in this dataset 50,3% females and 49,7% males. There is uneven distribution of both parents present versus single parents. 81.4% of students have both parents and 18.6% of students live in single-parent households.

Parent’s Highest Education Level and Students' Overall Scores¶

Based on this wikipedia article I have changed educational level of parents for better understanding and easier categorization:

From the violin chart we can see the distribution of overall scores in each parent's highest education category. We can see the slightly positive trend of students overall scores - students scores increase with parents educational level. An interesting observation from the chart is the large range of scores for parents educational level 4 (post-secondary non-tertiary education). Positive trend might not be as obvious from level to level, yet if we compare the first violin plot (ISCED 0) and last violin plot (ISCED 6) the difference is more distinct. First violin plot is wider at the bottom, indicating a larger proportion of students received lower scores. From the chart I can conclude that higher parents education level can have a positive impact on students' school scores.

Correlation between Overall Score and Home Possessions¶

As PISA 2012 Technical reports states Collecting information about households' possessions of family wealth are believed to capture wealth better than income because they reflect a more stable source of wealth. The image below shows the wording of items and their allocation for the following indices:

  • WEALTH : Wealth
  • CULTPOS : Cultural Possessions
  • HEDRES: Home educational Resources
  • HOMEPOS: Home Possessions

As mentioned at the beginning, the PISA 2012 dataset has many data points and plotting correlation between variables with a large number of data points can be illegible. For this reason I choose to plot 3 different scatter plots in order to highlight correlation between home possessions and overall students scores.

From the correlation matrix plot we can see slight positive correlation between home possession and overall score. Sometimes the Pearson coefficient itself does not provide clear results and interpretation can be a bit misleading sometimes. From the Scatter plot matrix above the correlation between home possessions and overall scores is not very clear. The first plot contains 5% of all data points, so the correlation is a bit more clear, yet not completely. In the second plot I plotted a regression line that indicates a slight positive relationship between variables. In the third plot I plotted variables in a 2D scatter plot or heat map with a clear distinction of where the majority of data points lie. From the charts we can see that students that come from a household with a higher index of home possession tend to have slightly higher scores.
